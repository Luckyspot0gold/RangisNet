## 6. Economic Cognition & Decision Making

### 6.1 Bounded Rationality and Information Overload

**Key Finding**: Humans have limited cognitive capacity; more information doesn't always improve decisions.

**Reference**:
- Simon, H. A. (1955). "A behavioral model of rational choice." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 69(1), 99-118.

**Relevance to AEAS**: Justifies the cognitive load management and progressive onboarding (Onboarding Resonance).

### 6.2 Affect Heuristic in Financial Decisions

**Key Finding**: Emotional "gut feelings" drive economic choices more than rational analysis.

**Reference**:
- Slovic, P., Finucane, M. L., Peters, E., & MacGregor, D. G. (2007). "The affect heuristic." *European Journal of Operational Research*, 177(3), 1333-1352.

**Relevance to AEAS**: Explains why "feeling" market state (haptic/audio) can improve decision speed without sacrificing accuracy.

### 6.3 Embodied Cognition in Financial Markets

**Key Finding**: Physical sensations influence abstract economic judgments (e.g., weight affects perceived value).

**Reference**:
- Jostmann, N. B., Lakens, D., & Schubert, T. W. (2009). "Weight as an embodiment of importance." *Psychological Science*, 20(9), 1169-1174.

**Relevance to AEAS**: Theoretical support for haptic pressure representing economic momentum.

## 9. Frequency & Harmonic Theory

### 9.1 The 432 Hz Controversy

**Key Finding**: 432 Hz is proposed as a "natural" tuning frequency; scientific evidence is mixed.

**Reference**:
- Calamassi, D., & Pomponi, G. P. (2019). "Music tuned to 440 Hz versus 432 Hz and the health effects: A double-blind cross-over pilot study." *Explore*, 15(4), 283-290.

**Relevance to AEAS**: While 432 Hz's health benefits are debated, its use as a stable baseline for harmonic ratios is mathematically valid.

### 9.2 Pythagorean Tuning and Whole Number Ratios

**Key Finding**: Simple frequency ratios (2:1, 3:2, 4:3) are perceived as consonant across cultures.

**Reference**:
- Burns, E. M. (1999). "Intervals, scales, and tuning." *The Psychology of Music* (2nd ed.), 215-264.

**Relevance to AEAS**: Justifies the 7-Bell system's use of integer multiples of 216 Hz (216, 288, 360, 432, 504, 648, 864).

### 9.3 Frequency Modulation and Human Perception

**Key Finding**: Humans are highly sensitive to FM; can detect modulation depth as low as 1%.

**Reference**:
- Moore, B. C., & Sek, A. (1996). "Detection of frequency modulation at low modulation rates: Evidence for a mechanism based on phase locking." *The Journal of the Acoustical Society of America*, 100(4), 2320-2331.

**Relevance to AEAS**: Supports using FM depth to represent volatility (subtle tremolo for low volatility, wide vibrato for high).
